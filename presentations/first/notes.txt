My thesis focuses on the design and implementation of an LLM red-teaming toolkit. So far I have designed a toolkit based on the shortcomings of existing ones.
===== MOTIVATION ====
Graph of interest in artificial intelligence -> AI usage skyrockets -> more and more smaller teams using AIs in projects
The numbers represent relative interest in searches, with a value of 100 representing the highest possible popularity of the term.
EU AI act is a regulation on AI, making red-teaming a mandatory practice -> a lot of smaller devs making AI apps -> need for a lightweight open-source red-teaming framework
Existing frameworks exist, all have some down-sides:
- Python Risk Identification Toolkit PyRIT(Microsoft): Enterprise-grade safety evaluation, doesn't easily support attack modules,
     attacks are embedded in a scenario-driven pipeline (to add a attack we would need to define a scenario and implement orchestration logic)
     this results in slower iteration and is over-kill for a small team
- MAD-MAX(Schoepf): Used for validating attack concepts and producing academic results rather than safety evaluation.
    Slower testing and expensive with API backends. Optimizes for experiments, not users.
- Garak(Nvidia): lightweight vulnerability scanner, allowing only single-turn prompt attacks. Modern attacks are mostly multi-turn conversations.
    Extensibility and modification is difficult as different modules are too intertwined (adding a new attack may mean rewriting the judge module).
===== Design ====
Experiment manifest (JSON or YAML files) is a specification/config file for configuring the target model(s), attack generator(s), the judging pipeline
and thresholds, and execution parameters (seeds, batch sizes, rate limits, output paths).
The orchestrator is central to the toolkit, it communicates with the attack module, requesting candidate adversarial prompts.
The orchestrator then delivers them to the backend model module, which generates a response that is sent back to the orchestrator.
The orchestrator then sends the model response to the judge module, where it is evaluated.
The evaluation is sent to the orchestrator in the case of multi-turn attacks, where it can be forwarded to the attack module.
Meanwhile everything is being logged in the JSONL format.
The JSONL logs will be visualized in a graphical interface for better clarity.






Hello, my name is Adam Veselý, and I am presenting my bachelor’s thesis supervised by Ing. Jakub Reš.

My thesis focuses on the design of a lightweight red-teaming toolkit for large language models.

Over the last few years, the use of large language models has grown rapidly.
Today, they are not only used by large companies, but also by small teams and individual developers, often as part of real-world applications.

At the same time, safety concerns are increasing.
Large language models can generate harmful or unsafe content when prompted adversarially, and with the adoption of the EU AI Act, systematic risk assessment and red-teaming will become mandatory for high-risk AI systems starting in 2026.

This creates a gap: red-teaming is becoming a requirement, but many existing tools are either too complex, too expensive, or not designed for smaller teams.

Several frameworks already exist. PyRIT, developed by Microsoft, is well suited for broad risk assessment and policy compliance testing, but it is less focused on adversarial optimisation, multi-turn attacks, and controlled experimental comparison. MAD-MAX focuses on automated jailbreak research, but is computationally expensive and primarily aimed at academic experiments. garak is lightweight, but mainly supports single-turn attacks and is harder to extend for more complex scenarios.

Based on these limitations, I designed a toolkit that focuses on reproducibility, modularity, and multi-turn red-teaming, while remaining lightweight enough to run on local hardware.

The core of the design is an experiment-driven architecture. Each experiment is declared using a manifest that specifies the target model, attack generator, judging pipeline, and execution parameters. An orchestrator then coordinates the execution of attacks, model inference, and evaluation.

The system is built from clearly separated components. Model adapters provide a uniform interface to different backends, including local models, API-based models, and custom research runtimes. Attack generators encapsulate adversarial strategies and support both single-turn and multi-turn attacks. Outputs are evaluated using a hybrid judging pipeline that combines heuristics, classifiers, and optional LLM-based judges.

All events and results are recorded in an append-only JSONL log, which serves as a single source of truth for reproducibility, auditing, and later analysis. A lightweight reporting interface then allows inspection and comparison of experiment outcomes.

In future work, the toolkit can be extended with more advanced automated attack generators, stronger judge calibration using human annotation, and broader support for agent-based or tool-using language models. Another important direction is large-scale empirical evaluation across multiple model families.

In summary, this thesis proposes a lightweight, extensible, and reproducible red-teaming toolkit that complements existing frameworks by focusing on adversarial experimentation and practical usability for smaller teams.
